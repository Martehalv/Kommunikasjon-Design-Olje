Beneath the Surface:
Understanding Norway’s Offshore Oil Work

How Brede (50) and his crew kept the wells alive far out at sea.

Far out in the North Sea, surrounded by nothing but wind and open water, hundreds of people live and work for weeks at a time on large offshore vessels. These ships form a vital part of Norway’s oil and gas industry, maintaining the wells and pipelines that keep the country’s energy production running.

One of those workers is Brede, who spent seven years on board the vessel Island Wellserver, a specialized ship built for servicing and repairing oil wells on the seabed. While on board between 2011 and 2018, he helped ensure that the flow of oil and gas remained steady and safe.

“I started as an RLWI operator, which is working directly with the equipment used on the seabed. And in 2015, I became the shift leader on board,” he says.

RLWI stands for Riserless Light Well Intervention – a method for performing maintenance and repairs directly on the seabed.

Life on board

Each mission lasted two weeks at sea, followed by four weeks on land. The Island Wellserver typically carried between 60 and 70 crew members.

“It was a very social life on the ship. Sometimes maybe a little too social… if you didn’t like someone, you couldn’t just walk away,” Brede says, laughing.

“But mostly it was good, everyone worked hard and we trusted each other.”

The crew’s main task was to maintain and test the systems that control the oil wells, both above the water (topside) and below it (subsea).

“We used hydraulic oil and other fluids to operate the tools at the bottom, and we also worked with electronics and computers to monitor everything. The main goal was to make sure the wells functioned properly, and that oil and gas could be produced safely,” he explains.

How oil is extracted from the seabed

To most of us, the process of pulling oil from deep beneath the ocean floor may seem complex. Brede explains it simply:

“First, a drilling rig makes a hole through the seabed. The hole can go as far as 5000 or 6000 meters deep. Then we insert pipes and steel casings to make it safe and stable. When we find where the oil is located, we make small, controlled holes in that layer of rock.”

“There’s so much pressure down there that the oil rises on its own,” Brede says. “But we control it carefully with valves and pumps to make sure nothing goes wrong.”

“When the oil reaches the surface, it goes through a quick cleaning process. What comes up can be a mix of oil, water, and sand, so we do a light separation before sending it off for proper treatment and storage.”

A ship built for precision

The Island Wellserver functions like a floating industrial complex. It includes:

  • A bridge for navigation
  • An engine room
  • Living quarters
  • A mess hall
  • A large deck for heavy equipment
  • A control room filled with screens and sensors.

“Most operations are managed from the control room. We monitor pressure, flow and safety systems from there. But we also check equipment directly. Some things have to be inspected daily, while others weekly. But honestly, there’s always something that needs attention.”

The ship doesn’t work alone.

“We always coordinate with engineers and logistics staff on land,” he explains. “We also work with nearby rigs and platforms. It’s a big system, and everything has to work together.”

Safety and responsibility

Working offshore means dealing with risk. But on Norwegian vessels, safety is taken extremely seriously.

“Norway has some of the world’s best safety systems,” Brede says firmly.

“We trained regularly for every possible situation. If anything serious happened, there was always a helicopter ready to help nearby.”

During his seven years at sea, Brede never experienced any major accidents, but the work was still dangerous.

“Working high up on the ship was one of the biggest risks,” he recalls.

“We often had to repair large pieces of equipment before lowering them into the sea, which meant climbing tall structures or cranes on deck. You always had to be careful.”

Drift Off

The crew’s most practiced emergency drill was called a ‘Drift Off’.

“That’s when the ship’s engines or positioning system fail while we’re still connected to the seabed with cables,” Brede explains. “If that happens, we have to react immediately… if those cables snap, it can create serious safety hazards, like risk of disconnection or uncontrolled movement of equipment. And it would be costly too... Everyone knew their roles in that kind of emergency.”

The technology behind the industry

The technology used in offshore oil work is highly advanced and constantly improving.

“It’s developed a lot, even since I left,” Brede says. “I think there’s more use of fiber optics and batteries on the seabed now. That’s more efficient than sending power through long cables. Everything is becoming more electric and automated.”

The systems are designed to withstand extreme conditions: freezing temperatures, heavy water pressure, violent storms, and the natural forces of the sea.

“It’s fascinating that everything keeps working down there,” he says. “It shows how advanced the engineering really is.”

The future of offshore work

Despite rapid technological change, Brede believes that ships like the Island Wellserver will continue to play a key role.

That ship doesn’t drill new wells, it keeps the existing ones alive.

That kind of maintenance will always be needed. In the future, we might have fewer platforms, and more pipelines that send oil directly to land. But we’ll still need people to monitor and maintain it all.

The work might change, but it won’t disappear.

A world beneath the sea

After years of working offshore, Brede has seen a side of Norway that most people never will; how complex systems deep below the ocean are maintained and controlled to keep Norway’s energy industry running.

“People think we just make a lot of money out there… but it’s tough work, long hours, and far from home,” he says.

“You do it because it’s important work, and because you take pride in doing it right.”

He smiles. “It’s strange, really. You can’t see any of it from land, but so much of what keeps our country running happens deep below the sea. And it all depends on people who make sure it works.”
