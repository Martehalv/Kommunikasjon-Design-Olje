Beneath the Surface:
Understanding Norway’s Offshore Oil Work

How Brede and his crew kept the wells alive far out at sea.

One of those workers is Brede, who spent seven years on board the vessel Island Wellserver, a specialized ship built for servicing and repairing oil wells on the seabed. While on board between 2011 and 2018, Brede helped ensure that the flow of oil and gas remained steady and safe.

“I started as an RLWI operator, which is working directly with the equipment used on the seabed. And in 2015, I became the shift leader on board,” he says.

RLWI stands for Riserless Light Well Intervention.

Life on board

Each mission lasted two weeks, which means being on board of the ship for two weeks at a time, and then getting two weeks off on land. The Island Well carried between 60 and 70 crew members at a time.

“It was a very social life on the ship. Sometimes maybe a little too social... if you didn’t like someone, you couldn’t just walk away,” Brede says laughing. “But mostly it was good, everyone worked hard and we trusted each other.”

The crew’s main task was to maintain and test the systems that control the oil wells, both above the water (topside) and below it (subsea).

“We used hydraulics, oil and other fluids to operate the tools at the bottom,” Brede explains. “We also worked with electronics and computers to monitor everything. The main goal was to make sure the wells functioned properly, and that oil and gas could be produced safely.”

How oil is extracted from the seabed ?

To most of us, the process of pulling oil from deep beneath the ocean floor may seem complex. Brede explains it simply:

“First, a drilling rig makes a hole through the seabed. The hole can go as far as 5000 or 6000 meters deep. Then we insert pipes and steel casings to make it safe and stable. When we find where the oil is located, we make small, controlled holes in that layer of rock.”

5000 m – 6000 m

“There’s so much pressure down there that the oil rises on its own,” Brede says. “But we control it carefully with valves and pumps to make sure nothing goes wrong.”

“When the oil reaches the surface, it goes through a quick cleaning process. What comes up can be a mix of oil, water, and sand, so we do a light separation before sending it off for proper treatment and storage.”

A ship built for precision

The Island Wellserver functions like a floating industrial complex. It includes:

  • A bridge for navigation
  • An engine room
  • Living quarters
  • A mess hall
  • A large deck for heavy equipment
  • A control room filled with screens and sensors.

“Most operations are managed from the control room. We monitor pressure, flow and safety systems from there. But we also check equipment directly. Some things have to be inspected daily, while others weekly. But honestly, there’s always something that needs attention.”

The ship doesn’t work alone.

“We always coordinate with engineers and logistics staff on land,” he explains. “We also work with nearby rigs and platforms. It’s a big system, and everything has to work together.”

Safety and responsibility

Working offshore means dealing with risk. But on Norwegian vessels, safety is taken extremely seriously.

“Norway has some of the world’s best safety systems,” Brede says firmly.

“We trained regularly for every possible situation. If anything serious happened, there was always a helicopter ready to help nearby.”

During his seven years at sea, Brede never experienced any major accidents, but the work was still dangerous.

“Working high up on the ship was one of the biggest risks,” he recalls.

“We often had to repair large pieces of equipment before lowering them into the sea, which meant climbing tall structures or cranes on deck. You always had to be careful.”
